# Shanzhai: Deconstruction in China

Han's argument in five chapters, each organized around a Chinese character: Chinese thought is deconstructionist *from the outset* — not a Western philosophical move applied to an alien tradition, but the native shape of Far Eastern thinking. The book uses the contemporary shanzhai (counterfeit/fake) phenomenon as its entry point, then traces the deep cultural logic behind it.

## Chapters

### Quan (權) — Law

*Quan* literally means the sliding weight on a scale: moveable, adjustable, provisional. As the root of the Chinese words for rights, intellectual property, and copyright, it inscribes relativity and situativity into those concepts from the start. Han contrasts this with the Western metaphysics of Being — Platonic truth as immutable, Hegelian substance, the god who "stands for truth and truthfulness." Chinese thought substitutes the *sliding weight of quan* for the *gravitation of Being*; situation replaces substance.

Hegel's accusation that the Chinese have an innate tendency to lie is his misreading of anti-essentialism. What looks like moral abandonment from within a metaphysics of fixed identity is simply the pragmatism of a thought that trusts no invariable essences.

### Zhen Ji (真跡) — Original

*Zhen ji* means "authentic trace" — and the structure of that phrase undoes the Western concept of the original. A trace does not condense into a monomorphic presence; it differs from itself, keeps the artwork from achieving a final form. Chinese masterpieces are palimpsests: connoisseurs and collectors inscribe themselves into the work via seals and commentaries. The oeuvre of a master is perpetually transformed — forgeries fill it, works disappear from it, retrospective taste shapes what counts as authentic. *The origin turns out to be a retrospective construction.*

Case study: Chang Dai-chien, the twentieth century's most celebrated Chinese painter, whose career was built on forgeries of Old Masters that were arguably more authentic than the originals. His "forgeries" of lost works known only from written descriptions created originals that had never existed. Han's conclusion: *the greater a master, the emptier his oeuvre* — a signifier without identity, loaded with new significance across eras.

Contrast with Adorno on Wagner: Adorno also sees the artwork as living and changing, but grounds its vitality in *inner depth and fullness*. The Chinese artwork changes because it is in itself *empty and flat* — the outwardness of tradition and situation drives change, not inward essence.

### Xian Zhan (閑章) — Seals of Leisure

Chinese seal stamps are not signatures in the Western sense. They do not finalize authorship or prohibit intervention; they *open a communicative field*. Art is sociable and communal — a farewell painting accumulates poems and seals from all present, each inscribing themselves into the work.

Han opposes two image-types: the Chinese **image of absence** (aperspective, asubjective, without soul or gaze) against the Western **image of presence** (Van Eyck's *Arnolfini Portrait* as paradigm — signature, date, convex mirror, the divine gaze, all consolidating authorial presence at the center). European signatures are *seals of finito*; Chinese stamps are invitations.

Benjamin's anecdote of the Chinese painter who disappears into his own painting illustrates the difference: the viewer empties himself and enters the picture precisely *because* the picture is inhabited by no one.

### Fuzhi (複製) — Copy

Two Chinese copy-concepts: *fangzhipin* (obvious imitation, museum-shop model) and *fuzhipin* (exact reproduction of equal value, no negative connotation). Western museums regularly misread the latter as deception.

The [[Ise Shrine]] example: the supreme Shinto sanctuary, considered 1,300 years old, is completely rebuilt every twenty years. UNESCO removed it from the World Heritage list — for UNESCO it is at most twenty years old. Han's inversion: *the copy is more original than the original*, since the older a building gets, the further it is from its original state. Reproduction is a technique of preservation; it is how identity persists *through* death rather than *against* it — the Buddhist logic of cycles over creatio ex nihilo.

The terra-cotta warriors: manufactured through modular mass production from the start. The *Manual of the Mustard Seed Garden* as painting-by-modules. Modular production opposes uniqueness in order to maximize reproducibility; it *modulates* the same to create difference, working like nature rather than like genius.

### Shanzhai (山寨) — Fake

Shanzhai (literally "mountain stronghold," after the outlaws of *Water Margin*) as the contemporary form of Chinese creative logic: not crude forgery but playful, transformative variation. Shanzhai phones are hardly inferior to Nokias and Samsungs in design; they add functions the originals lack. Labels mutate — Adidas becomes Adidos, Adadas, Adadis — in what Han calls a "Dadaist game" that parodies economic monopoly.

**Shanzhai is [[decreation]]**: it opposes *creatio ex nihilo* with process; Being with path; identity with transformational difference. It manifests the genuinely Chinese spirit. Maoism as shanzhai Marxism — transforming Marx's doctrine in the absence of an industrial proletariat. Chinese "both-and" versus Western "either-or": contradiction is not a Chinese concept.

Han closes with the possibility of *shanzhai democracy* — not as a utopian hope but as the logical extrapolation of a political system already showing hybrid characteristics.

## Key Concepts

- **[[Decreation]]** (*Ent-schöpfung*): Han's term for the desubstantializing operation that empties out Being. Not destruction but the removal of essence; what remains is process and trace.
- **[[The Trace]]** (*ji*, 跡): The unit of the Chinese original. Neither presence nor sign in the Western sense; it keeps the work open to continual rearrangement, like Freud's memory-traces.
- **[[Process vs Being]]**: The organizing contrast of the book. Western metaphysics is founded on Being (immutable, monomorphic, self-identical); Chinese thought is founded on process (the Tao, continuous change, no absolute beginning).
- **[[Emptiness]]** (*kong*): Not nihilistic nothingness (Hegel's misreading) but the negativity of *decreation* and *absence* — it desubstantializes Being and opens the work to inscription.
- **[[Image of Presence vs Image of Absence]]**: Western images consolidate authorial subjectivity at the center (signature, gaze, soul); Chinese images are aperspective and asubjective, inviting the viewer's dissolution.
