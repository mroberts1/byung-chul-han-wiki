The Chinese concept of *zhen ji* (真跡) — literally "authentic trace" — is Han's key for dismantling the Western concept of the original. The trace is not a copy of a prior presence, nor a sign pointing toward a stable referent. It *differs from itself*: it keeps the artwork perpetually open, preventing it from achieving a final form or condensing into a monomorphic presence.

Han draws an explicit parallel to Freud's memory-traces: not stable representations of experience, but products of complex construction subject to continual rearrangement and transcription. New constellations alter the appearance of earlier traces; past, present, and future interfuse. The Chinese original shares this structure — it is not a unique creation event but an *unending process of rearrangement*.

## What the trace implies

The trace **effaces artistic subjectivity**: there is no creator whose presence underwrites the work's authenticity. Instead, the work opens itself to collective inscription — connoisseurs' seals, collectors' commentaries, the poetry of friends at farewell gatherings. The work is a *palimpsest*.

The trace also **deconstructs originality**: if the original is always already a trace — processual, differential, subject to retrospective construction — then the distinction between original and copy collapses. The "original" is what a given era constructs as original from the traces available.

## Relation to the image of absence

The trace is the formal structure behind Han's [[Image of Presence vs Image of Absence|image of absence]]. Chinese images are structurally tracial: they have no *adyton* (the sealed inner sanctum of Western sacred space), nothing that shuts off or holds itself apart. They are open on all sides — penetrable — precisely because no stabilizing presence inhabits them.
