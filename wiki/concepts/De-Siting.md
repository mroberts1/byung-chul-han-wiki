Han's term for the primary operation of globalization on culture. De-siting (*Ent-Ortung*) is not merely the movement of cultural forms from one place to another — that would be ordinary cultural exchange. De-siting dissolves the *site* (*Ort*) as such: it de-internalizes the here and now, strips culture of its spatial-historical *embeddedness*, and makes what was rooted freely mobile.

## The site in the strong sense

For Heidegger — Han's primary interlocutor here — the site is where Being gathers auratic depth. The "here and now" that cannot be repeated *there* is the condition of [[De-Auratization|aura]] (Benjamin). The site is the tip of the spear that gathers everything into itself; it gives Being its "soul." Heidegger's world is populated by things (jug, ploughshare, heron, cross) whose monosyllabic simplicity enacts a deliberate counter-modernity: the site as bulwark against dispersal.

De-siting undoes this. When cultural forms detach from their ritual or historical context and enter the hypercultural side by side, the auratic "here and now" gives way to site-less repetition. What was unique and unrepeatable becomes a freely available element in the hypermarket of culture.

## De-siting and de-facticization

Heidegger's concept of *thrownness* (*Geworfenheit*) names the way Dasein finds itself always already thrown into a particular inheritance — site, language, tradition, body, mortality. De-siting is simultaneously *de-facticization*: it releases Dasein from this thrownness, expands the space of possible projections beyond "the possibility it has inherited." [[Homo Liber]] — the free human of hypercultural existence — is de-facticized Dasein.

Han's ambivalence: this is both loss (the warmth of the site, the anchor of identity, narrative coherence) and gain (freedom, lightness, the AND-logic of [[Hyperculture]]). He refuses to simply mourn de-siting. De-sited, de-auratized cultures achieve "*another* Being, *another* reality."

## Heidegger as closet tourist

Han's sharpest move: Heidegger, who laments de-siting most bitterly, is himself a *tourist*. His famous collection of things (from "The Thing") is not an authentic expression of peasant rootedness but a *romantic counter-world projected onto* peasant life. Like the romantic tourist who seeks a primitive, untouched There, Heidegger is on pilgrimage to an imaginary destination. His "threshold" — which "petrifies pain" — is the figure of *homo dolores*: the suffering native who won't cross over into hypercultural freedom.

## Pilgrims, wanderers, tourists

Three figures of movement in the hypercultural landscape:
- **The pilgrim**: driven by the Here/There asymmetry; the world must be orderly; footprints must be preserved; modernity's dominant figure (Bauman)
- **Nietzsche's wanderer**: moves in a de-teleologized world, capable of *hypervision*; still melancholic, still a via dolorosa
- **The hypercultural tourist**: the Here/There asymmetry is dissolved; There is only another Here; no longing, no fear; culture as *cul-tour*
