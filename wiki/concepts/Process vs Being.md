The organizing contrast of Han's *Shanzhai* and a structuring opposition throughout his work. Western metaphysics is founded on **Being** (*Sein*, *ousia*): what is permanent, self-identical, underlying all change as that which remains the same. This gives Western thought its characteristic commitments — to the original as unique creation, to truth as immutable correspondence, to the subject as stable selfhood, to law as invariant rule.

Chinese thought is founded on **process**: the Tao ("the way") embraces change where essence resists it. There is no absolute beginning, no creation in the emphatic sense, no discontinuous rupture. Time moves as continuous transformation, not as a series of events. *Ruins* — monuments to founding ruptures — are inconceivable within this framework.

## The pragmatism of process

Han describes Chinese thought as *pragmatic* in a specific sense: it traces not essences but the changeable constellations of things (*pragmata*). The task is to recognize the course of things, correlate situationally with it, and derive benefit — not to reveal an underlying truth. Power (*quan li*) belongs not to subjectivity but to situativity.

This is what Hegel misread as immorality: in the absence of fixed essences, commitments look provisional, and constancy looks impossible. But provisional is not arbitrary — *quan* always seeks balance, not chaos.

## In relation to [[Decreation]]

Process is what remains after [[Decreation|decreation]] has desubstantializing Being. The path (Tao) is Being emptied of essence — flat, without depth or mystery, continually changing course. Heidegger also uses the image of the path, but his paths *deepen* and stop abruptly at the untrodden. The Taoist path keeps going.
