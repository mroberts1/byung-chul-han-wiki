Han's opposition between two fundamentally different regimes of the image, developed through close reading of specific artworks in *Shanzhai*.

## Image of Presence

Paradigm: Van Eyck's *Arnolfini Portrait* (1434).

The painting consolidates authorial presence through multiple simultaneous operations: the signature ("Johannes de Eyck fuit hic"), the date, the convex mirror showing the painter as witness among witnesses, the Passion medallions linking the mirror to the eye of Christ. The demonstrative *hic* ("was here") creates a referential structure that anchors the creator's presence to a specific moment and place. The signature functions as a *seal of finito* — it closes and prohibits intervention. The picture's "true setting is the soul."

Western images are perspectival and subjective: they project from and return to a centered subjectivity.

## Image of Absence

Chinese images are aperspective and asubjective. No authorial presence inhabits them. The painter's seal does not establish presence — it opens a communicative field. The work is a *trace* without a center; it invites inscription rather than prohibiting it.

Han's illustration: Walter Benjamin's anecdote (from *Berlin Childhood around 1900*) of a Chinese painter who disappears into his own picture, follows the footpath to the cottage door, pauses, smiles, and vanishes. The primary experience of the image is not *Vorstellung* (representation by a subject) but *mimetic distortion* — a contemplative emptying of the subject that allows entry into the picture. The image can be entered precisely because no one inhabits it.

## Structural relation to [[The Trace|the trace]] and [[Emptiness|emptiness]]

The image of absence is structurally tracial ([[The Trace]]): differential, open, without adyton. Its [[Emptiness|emptiness]] is not lack but the condition of its communal, processual life. The more famous a Chinese painting, the more inscriptions it carries — fame increases transformation, not fixity.
